## Responsibilities List


#### The responsibilities of the safety captain include:

-   Informing others about the importance of safety.
-   Encouraging others to be conscious of safety.
-   Making sure members know if they have been unsafe, and how to improve to be safer.
-   Conducting a safety inspection in work areas, including the pit station during competition.
-   Reading and becoming familiar with safety regulations and emergency procedures.
-   If given the chance, be able to show advisors at events your safety program.

#### The responsibilities of the team members include:

-   Reading and understanding this safety manual.
-   Maintaining a safe and productive work environment.
-   Attending the team required safety training.
-   Reminding teammates of safety rules if they are endangering themselves or others.
-   Reporting to the Safety Captain if safety issues arise.

#### The responsibilities of the mentors include:

-   Lead by example. Practice the same safety behaviors that we expect from the students.
-   Provide guidance and ensure a safe environment, including proper tool usage and personal protective equipment, safeguards, and other safety equipment as required.
-   Stage leadership as outlined in the Hand Tools section.
-   Offer safety or design considerations to the team.
-   Eliminate or consult with relevant event safety and restrictions by reading the 'At the Events' section 4 of the FIRST Robotics Competition Administrative Manual and 'Site Info' for your event(s).
-   Collect and store Material Safety Data Sheets (MSDS) for any chemicals, paint, and batteries, etc. the team uses.
  

## Machine Shop Overview and General Safety


Safety is one of the most important parts of being on our FRC team. At our 2064 Panther Project meetings, we make it a priority to keep every team member—whether student, mentor, or parent—safe throughout all phases of our robotics season. This includes during team meetings, building, testing, traveling, and competing. We all have a responsibility to lead by example by acting safely and responsibly at all times. This means wearing the right safety gear, avoiding loose clothing or jewelry, and tying back long hair. We always need to be aware of our surroundings and speak up if something looks unsafe. Food and drinks are not allowed in the workspace to prevent contamination and damage.

  

## Safety and Health Training Checklist & First Aid Kit


Before starting any task in the workshop, it’s important to check your surroundings and equipment to make sure everything is safe. Make sure the area is clean and well-lit, and that there are not any tripping hazards on the floor. Personal protective equipment (PPE) should always be worn when needed, and proper clothing—like closed-toe shoes and no baggy clothes or jewelry—should be worn. All tools should be in good condition and used on sturdy surfaces like benches or vises. Safety guards should be in place where needed, and soldering irons should always be stored in their holders when not in use. Our first aid kit should be stocked with up-to-date supplies. These quick checks keep everyone safe and help prevent accidents.

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)


While participating in FIRST, whether it be at the workshop building the robot or at the competition, it is crucial that all team members and spectators have PPE (Personal Protective Equipment) at the right time. This section will cover all of the PPE you'll need.

PPE is the gear that keeps us safe while working. This includes safety glasses, gloves, earplugs, and more. Eye protection is required when doing anything that could send particles flying, such as drilling, cutting, or soldering. During competitions, safety glasses are mandatory in the pits and practice fields. If you wear prescription glasses, you’ll need either rated goggles or safety glasses with side shields. Gloves should be worn when working with heat or electricity, but never around spinning machines. For loud tools or tasks, hearing protection like earplugs is required. Only closed-toe shoes are allowed in the shop—no sandals, flip flops, or heels.

  

#### Eye and Face Protection

You must wear eye protection when:

-   Working on the robot including grinding, drilling, soldering, cutting, welding etc.
-   When there is a risk of exposure to flying particles or chemical exposure is present.


At FIRST events you must wear eye protection when:

-   In the pit station
-   On the playing field
-   On the practice field
-   Any area posted with signs requiring the use of eye protection.


Safety Glasses Requirements:

-   Wear ANSI-approved, UL Listed, or CSA rated non-shaded safety glasses.
-   Lightly tinted yellow, rose, blue, and amber tints are FIRST approved. Reflective lenses are prohibited.
-   Protective eyewear must be clearly visible to others.
-   Protective eyewear must be worn over non-safety rated prescription glasses
-   If you wear safety related eye glasses, you can use side shields.

#### Hearing Protection

Make hearing protection devices available, such as earplugs, wherever noise levels exceed sound limits. Consider 85 decibels as a threshold. At events, hearing protection is available at the Pit Administration. A Mentor can provide assistance in evaluating high-noise tasks and determining appropriate hearing protection devices.

## Safety and Health Best Practices


Keeping our workspace clean and organized helps avoid accidents like slips and trips. When we move the robot, we should use the team’s robot cart to avoid injury. If lifting the robot by hand, make sure all parts are secure and always lift with your legs, not your back. It takes two people to lift the robot safely. During testing, everyone should stay behind safety lines and keep clear of the robot while it's powered on. These habits keep everyone safer and make our workspace more efficient.

#### General Safety:

-   Maintain a healthy attitude regarding safety.
-   Please reference FIRST Robotics Competition Team Safety Manual - Appendix
-   Always be mindful and thoughtful in the work area.
-   Safety glasses are to be worn by all people who are working and near tools and robots.
-   Proper clothing is to be worn; no open toed shoes while working.
-   Always wear a bump cap when handling sharp or pointed objects.
-   Lift with your legs, not your back, to prevent injury.
-   Loose items (like tools) should not be on the floor as they can provide a tripping hazard.
-   Sharp objects and tools used for cutting should be properly put away to prevent cuts and lacerations.
-   Objects jutting out into the workspace shall be marked with bright, easily seen colors to prevent people from walking into them.
-   The workspace should be free of any sort of debris that could cause accidents and disorganization.
-   Always walk and work in a controlled and thoughtful manner. Maintain complete control of the robot at all times.
-   Never make any unauthorized changes to the robot without permission from a mentor or captain.
-   Take special consideration into your safety when working above normal height. Always fully open a ladder and never stand on a non-approved step.
-   Follow safe work practices, including safe use of all tools and protective equipment (safety glasses, shoes, gloves, hearing protection, etc).
-   Be especially careful around high-speed rotating components, both on and off the robot. If you are putting a high-speed rotating component on the robot, make sure the component is designed to be used the way you are using it.


#### Mechanical Guards

A guard is an object that protects the user from moving parts of tools. Parts of tools that reciprocate or move must be guarded. Never remove a guard when a tool is being used.

Safety guards for power tools must be used where required. Never use any equipment without proper safety guards. Notify your Safety Captain and Mentor of any broken or defective equipment use the Lock Out Tag Out (LOTO) process and take it out of service until repairs are made.

Guards must be supplied to the user to protect them and others from:

-   Points of operation
-   Nip points
-   Rotating parts
-   Flying chips and sparks

  

#### Foot Protection

When engaged in FIRST activities, FIRST Robotics Competition participants must wear shoes that completely cover the entire foot. Shoes must be substantial and close-toed to protect against foot injuries, regardless of work location. Flip-flops, sandals, mules, lightweight slippers, etc. are not acceptable when working on or near the robot.

Spectators attending FIRST competitions should follow these footwear rules. If substantial close-toed shoes are not available, they may not enter the pit area.

  

#### Transporting the Robot

Make sure the robot is stable on the cart before transporting. Keep the cart under control at all times and ensure that the cart will not present a hazard.

Use the gate opening when entering/exiting the playing field. Make sure that at least one member of the team is controlling the cart at any time.

## General Shop Safety


Our shop should always be clean, well-lit, and free from hazards. We need to keep all floors clear and avoid creating tripping zones. Materials should never be stacked higher than shoulder height, and anything that looks unstable should be moved or fixed. Unsafe tools or environments should be reported to a mentor immediately. Keeping things tidy helps prevent accidents before they happen.

#### Lock Out Tag Out (LOTO) Process:

-   After identifying the piece of equipment that needs service or maintenance, review energy sources of the machine to ensure that they are turned off.
-   Disconnect all power sources by turning off circuit breakers, closing valves and/or unplugging the equipment. Drain, block or bleed out any stored energy (i.e. compressed air).
-   Apply appropriate labels and notify a mentor that maintenance is required.
-   Once maintenance work is complete, verify all parts are in place and safe and return to use.

  

## Power and Hand Tool Safety
---

When using power tools or hand tools, always wear your PPE and choose the right tool for the job. Never use tools that are broken, dull, or defective. If a tool isn’t safe, label it and report it to a mentor using the Lock Out Tag Out (LOTO) process. Tools should only be used on strong, stable surfaces like workbenches or vises. Always cut away from your body and check that no one is in your cutting path. Tools should be stored with all guards or covers in place to avoid injury.

#### Power Tool Safety:

Prevent hazards with power tools by sticking to the following rules:

-   Don't carry tools around by the cord or wire; be safe with cords and never pull or yank them
-   Be sure to keep cords away from all other objects to prevent entanglement
-   People that are not working with the tools should be at a safe distance away as to not get hurt by flying debris
-   Make sure you have both your hands operating the tool at once, in other words, keep hands off trigger of power tools when not in use, take care of your tools for max performance
-   Have good footing and balance when operating a tool
-   NO loose clothing should be worn when using a power tool, these can easily get caught
-   Make sure others know if a power tool is broken, label it with a sign saying "do not use"

*Appropriate personal protective equipment such as safety goggles and gloves must be worn to protect against hazards that may be encountered while using tools. Tools shall not be issued or used unless kept in a safe condition. Tools shall be inspected regularly and maintained in a safe condition. Tools shall be kept clean and dry and stored properly. The types of power tools are determined by their power source: electric, pneumatic, liquid fuel, hydraulic, and powder-actuated.

  

#### Hand Tools

Hand tools are tools that are powered manually. Hand tools include anything from axes to wrenches. The greatest hazards posed by hand tools result from misuse and improper maintenance. Some examples include the following:

-   If a chisel is used as a screwdriver, the tip of the chisel may break and fly off, hitting the user or other participants.
-   If a wooden handle on a tool, such as a hammer or an axe, is loose, splintered, or cracked, the head can fly off and strike the user or other participants.
-   If impact tools such as chisels, wedges, or drift pins have mushroomed heads, the heads might shatter on impact, sending sharp fragments flying toward the user or other participants.
-   Unsafe hand tools shall not be issued or used by participants.

Participants should be trained in the proper use and handling of tools. Direct the tool's sharp edge away from other participants working close by. Knives and scissors must be sharp because dull tools can cause more hazards than sharp ones.

-   Cracked saw blades must be removed from service.
-   Wrenches must not be sprung. The jaws are sprung to the point that slippage occurs.
-   Iron or steel hand tools may produce sparks that can be an ignition source around flammable substances. Spark-resistant tools should be used where flammable gases, highly volatile liquids, and other explosive substances are stored or used.

## Soldering Safety


Soldering can be dangerous if not done correctly. Always wear eye protection and never touch the hot part of the soldering iron. Use a fire-resistant work surface and keep the iron in its holder when not in use. Make sure the area is well-ventilated. After soldering, throw away any waste properly and wash your hands thoroughly to avoid lead exposure or contamination.

Soldering can be dangerous because of the heat from the iron and the chemical fumes and vapors released from the solder and flux respectively. When soldering, observe the following points:

-   Use lead-free solder only and solder with electrically heated soldering iron/gun only.
-   No torches or open flames of any kind are allowed in event venues, except by personnel in specified areas (such as the event machine shop).
-   Wear eye and face protection.
-   Solder in well-ventilated areas.
-   Never touch the iron/gun. It heats to extreme temperatures that will cause severe burns.
-   Prevent burns by wearing cotton clothing that covers your arms and legs.
-   Always wash your hands with soap and water after handling solder.
-   Work on a fire-resistant surface.
-   Keep your soldering iron in its protective holder when not actually being used
-   Never leave a heated soldering iron unattended
-   Do not leave any hot tools where someone can accidentally contact the hot element.

## Electrical Safety


Working with electrical parts requires extra caution. Always let your teammates know when you’re working on a live robot or using electrical tools. Never work on the robot while it's energized unless supervised. Check wires and cords for any damage before plugging them in. Don’t overload power strips or connect extension cords to each other, which can cause overheating or fires. Communicate clearly to make sure everyone knows what’s happening around high-voltage areas.

  

Electrical Tools:

Electrical burns and shocks are the most common of these dangers. When using electrical tools, the following guidelines should always be followed:

-   Use gloves and appropriate footwear when an electrical tool is at use
-   Never keep electrical tools in wet or damp areas. Parts of electrical tools should never be near any wet place, especially when they are in use
-   The only time that an electrical tool should be operated in a damp or wet location is if they are approved for it
-   Make sure to have good lighting when operating an electrical tool
-   Cords should not be in the way of workspace to prevent tripping

## Fire Safety


Fires can happen, so we need to be prepared. Fire extinguishers are located throughout the shop (one in the closet and one outside our robotics closet door). In case of fire, alert an adult, follow the emergency procedures, and evacuate calmly to the nearest exit . Don’t block fire exits or extinguisher locations. Only use an extinguisher if it’s safe and you’ve been trained.

## Battery Safety


Batteries can be dangerous if damaged or used incorrectly. Batteries contain sulfuric acid (H2SO4), which is clear and colorless, and will burn skin on contact. Don’t use batteries that are cracked, swollen, or leaking. Battery acid is corrosive and can seriously hurt you. If you get battery acid on your skin, rinse it with water immediately and get medical help. If a battery leaks, pour baking soda on the spill to neutralize the acid and place the battery in a sealed container while wearing gloves and eye protection. Always charge batteries in a well-ventilated space and dispose of them properly to protect the environment.  
  
#### General Damaged Battery Information and Warnings

Any battery that is visibly damaged in any way is dangerous and unusable. Don't take a chance.

Here are other reasons you should not use a damaged battery:

-   It contains stored electrical energy that could cause the battery to rapidly heat up due to an internal electrical short circuit, and possibly explode.
-   The 12V batteries provided in your kit or in parts contain sulfuric acid that will burn human tissue on contact.

Set aside damaged battery and handle accordingly:

-   Wash contacted skin with a large quantity of water.
-   Seek medical treatment.
-   Periodically inspect your batteries for any signs of damage or leaking electrolyte.
-   The outer case of a battery may be cracked, but the crack may not be visible and might eventually leak electrolyte.
-   Treat it as a hazardous material and process it in accordance with the battery's MSDS.
-   Don't take chances- don't use it!

#### Necessary Safety Materials

IFRs recommend that teams keep the following items readily available whenever working with batteries:

-   A box of sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) to neutralize any exposed acid electrolyte.
-   A pair of acid-resistant rubber or plastic leak-proof gloves to wear when handling a defective battery.
-   A suitable non-metallic leak-proof container in which to place the defective battery.
-   Procedure for Handling a Leaking Battery

When an electrolyte leak occurs:

-   Neutralize it by pouring the sodium bicarbonate on all wetted surfaces. The bicarbonate of soda itself is not caustic and will react with the acid in the electrolyte leaving a residue that can be disposed of in a conventional manner such as rinsing with water.
-   Follow emergency handling instructions of the MSDS and notify Mentor.
-   Put on the gloves before handling the battery.
-   Place the battery in a leak-proof container for removal.
-   Be sure to neutralize any acid on the gloves before removing and storing them.
-   Seek medical attention if skin comes into contact with any chemical.
-   Properly dispose of the battery, which is now hazardous material.

#### At a FIRST event:

-   Immediately send the person in contact with acid to the First Aid Station/EMTs.
-   Report incident to the Pit Administration, Supervisor, so that the individual can fill out an official Incident Report Form.
-   Pit Administration will immediately contact Event Management for further instruction from Event and Venue Authorities.

#### Battery Disposal

Be sure to dispose of all batteries properly and safely. Most retailers of automotive batteries will accept and properly dispose of them at no cost.

#### Charging and Handling

-   Keep the battery charging area clean and orderly.
-   Place your battery charger where cooling air can freely circulate.
-   Be aware that battery chargers can fail without proper ventilation.
-   Do not short out the battery terminals. If metal tools/parts contact the terminals simultaneously, it will create a direct short circuit. This may cause high heat to develop in the battery terminal/part tool area and the battery could explode.
-   Do not charge the battery at greater than the manufacturer's maximum recommended rate.

#### Ongoing Battery Inspection

-   Periodically inspect your battery for any evidence of damage, such as a cracked case or leaking electrolyte.
-   Bent terminals can also be a potential leak source.
-   Inspect the battery before and after each round of competition.

  

## Robot Testing Safety


Testing the robot should only happen in approved areas with marked safety zones. Make sure everyone is clear of the robot, by calling out “All clear” before turning it on, and never run tests alone. Only trained drivers should control the robot during testing, and an emergency stop button should always be nearby. Safety always comes first—even when we’re excited to see the robot in action.

  
## Emergency Event Procedures


If there’s an emergency, call 911 immediately. Then provide care only if you’re trained in first aid. Notify a mentor right away and follow their instructions. If evacuation is needed, stay calm, exit the building, and head to the designated meeting point outside. Never go back inside until it’s safe to do so.

## Cybersecurity


Keeping our team’s digital information secure is just as important as physical safety. That means using strong passwords, keeping them private, and not sharing accounts. All team discussions should happen on approved platforms, and posts to social media must be approved. Use antivirus software and keep it updated. If you step away from your computer, use a lock screen. Be cautious about phishing emails by using the SLAM method: check the Sender, hover over Links, watch for suspicious Attachments, and read the Message carefully. If something seems off, don’t click it—report it to a mentor.

SLAM:

-   Sender – Is the address legit?  
-   Links – Hover to check where they lead.  
-   Attachments – Don’t open unexpected files.  
-   Message – Look out for suspicious tone or urgency.
