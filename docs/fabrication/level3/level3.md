# ⚙️ Precision Machining: Mill & Lathe Setup
> **Objective:** Learn the fundamental safety, setup, and operation procedures for the manual milling machine and metal lathe to produce high-precision custom shafts, spacers, and brackets.

While our CNC machines are great for cutting flat plates, some parts require manual, high-precision 3D machining. The manual mill and metal lathe are the most precise tools in our shop, but they are also among the most dangerous. 

!!! danger "No Gloves. No Sleeves. No Exceptions."
    **NEVER wear gloves when operating the mill or lathe.** If a glove catches on a spinning lathe chuck or end mill, it will pull your hand into the machine before you can hit the E-Stop. Long hair must be tied up, and long sleeves must be rolled tightly above the elbow. 

---

## 🔄 Step 1: The Metal Lathe (Turning)
The lathe spins the workpiece while a stationary cutting tool removes material. In FRC, we primarily use the lathe to turn down 1/2" hex shaft to round 1/2" (to fit inside bearings) and to cut snap ring (retaining ring) grooves.

**Standard Lathe Setup:**
1. **Workholding:** Secure your aluminum hex stock inside the 3-jaw chuck. Ensure at least 1 inch of stock is gripped tightly, and use a live center in the tailstock if your part sticks out more than 3 inches.
2. **Tool Post Setup:** Ensure the cutting tool is set exactly to the centerline of the workpiece. If it is too high or too low, it will rub instead of cut.
3. **Facing & Turning:** * *Facing:* Cutting across the end of the stock to make it perfectly flat.
    * *Turning:* Cutting along the side of the stock to reduce its diameter. Always take light passes (0.010" - 0.020") when roughing, and a final spring pass for a smooth finish.
4. **Cutting Grooves:** Use a specific parting or grooving tool to cut a 0.039" wide groove for standard 1/2" E-clips or snap rings.

---

## 🗜️ Step 2: The Manual Mill (Milling)
The mill holds the workpiece stationary while a spinning cutting tool (end mill) removes material. We use the mill for slotting, drilling precise hole patterns, and squaring up blocks of aluminum.

**Standard Mill Setup:**
1. **Workholding (The Vise):** Place your stock in the milling vise. Use **parallels** (precision ground steel bars) underneath the part to raise it up and ensure it sits perfectly flat. Tap the part down with a dead-blow hammer until the parallels cannot slide.
2. **Tooling & Collets:** Insert the correct end mill into the spindle using an R8 collet. Never tighten a collet without a tool inside it!
3. **The Edge Finder:** Use a mechanical edge finder to locate the exact X and Y edges of your stock. Once found, zero out your Digital Readout (DRO).
4. **Milling:** Lock the axes you are not moving. Feed the material against the rotation of the cutter (Conventional Milling) for the best control and finish.

---

## 🛢️ Step 3: Speeds, Feeds, and Coolant
Machining metal generates a massive amount of heat. If things get too hot, aluminum will actually melt and weld itself to your cutting tool (galling), ruining both the tool and the part.

* **RPM:** Aluminum requires high RPMs and fast feeds. Steel requires low RPMs and slow feeds. (Check the shop speed chart before turning on the spindle).
* **Coolant/Lubrication:** Always apply cutting fluid (like Tap Magic or WD-40) to the cutter and the workpiece when making heavy cuts in aluminum. 

---

## 🧹 Step 4: Cleanup & Maintenance
A dirty machine is an inaccurate machine. 

!!! warning "Put down the air hose!"
    **NEVER use compressed air to blow metal chips off the mill or lathe.** This forces tiny, sharp metal chips directly into the precision sliding ways and bearings, destroying the machine. 

1. Use a chip brush or shop vac to remove all metal shavings from the ways, chuck, and vise.
2. Remove your cutting tools and return them to their designated drawers.
3. Wipe down the sliding surfaces with a clean rag and a light coat of machine oil to prevent rust.
